Research Topics

Tentatively, the PI proposed the following topics to research on for his first term with MUST. Candidates who want to join ACCMaR are welcome to bring in their own ideas that are compatible with our research fields and resources. 

1.  AI for Materials Discovery

The integration of artificial intelligence into scientific research represents an irreversible and transformative trend. As science and technology continue to advance at an unprecedented pace, AI has dramatically enhanced our ability to process, analyze, and interpret large volumes of data. In materials science, in particular, the synergy between AI and data-driven approaches has accelerated the discovery of functional materials and holds the promise of enabling autonomous materials design, synthesis, and characterization within closed-loop workflows.

ACCMaR is committed to harnessing the power of AI to advance materials discovery. By integrating AI with electronic-structure theory and materials data, we aim to develop end-to-end pipelines that systematically extract knowledge from the scientific literature, identify physically meaningful descriptors, construct high-quality databases, and uncover structure–property relationships. Our current research interests focus on building and curating data resources for functional materials in catalysis, energy storage (e.g., batteries), and electronic and optical devices.

2.  Computational Materials

First-principles calculations provide direct access to chemical and physical properties at the microscopic scale, offering critical insights into the structure–property relationships of functional materials. In catalysis, in particular, physically meaningful descriptors derived from theoretical simulations—such as binding (free) energies—have been successfully correlated with experimental performance. These descriptors have enabled efficient and predictive screening of high-performance catalysts for desirable reactions, including the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), oxygen reduction and evolution reactions (ORR/OER), and carbon dioxide reduction reaction (CO2RR).

The effectiveness of such descriptors fundamentally depends on a detailed understanding of the underlying reaction mechanisms, which involve the coupled evolution of geometric and electronic structures of both reactants and catalytic materials. From a thermodynamic and statistical perspective, accurately identifying stable configurations of heterogeneous systems is a prerequisite for reliable modeling of reaction pathways and energetics.

ACCMaR aims to elucidate the mechanistic details governing catalytic reactions. Our approach integrates first-principles electronic-structure methods (e.g., plane-wave DFT frameworks), surrogate techniques such as machine-learning force fields, and sampling strategies (e.g., genetic algorithms) to model complex catalytic systems. In addition, we are particularly interested in non-equilibrium processes occurring during catalysis, which may be driven by electronic excitations and charge-transfer dynamics and therefore require more advanced electronic-structure methodologies beyond conventional ground-state approaches.

3.  Electronic Structure Methods

Building on the PI’s prior research experience at UCSB, ACCMaR will continue to advance the development of electronic-structure methods. Although density functional theory (DFT) has been widely and successfully applied in computational chemistry and materials science, it remains fundamentally limited in its ability to accurately describe certain classes of problems. These limitations are particularly evident for excited-state phenomena in heterogeneous systems.

Key excited-state properties of interest include charged and optical excitations, as well as charge transfer and charge transport processes. Representative heterogeneous systems include embedded motifs in condensed phases and adsorbates or dopants at material interfaces. In such cases, inadequate treatment of excited states or system heterogeneity can lead to substantial errors in predicted properties, undermining the reliability of computational results.

To better serve the computational chemistry and materials science communities, ACCMaR is dedicated to the development of post-DFT and post-self-consistent-field (post-SCF) methodologies for the accurate treatment of heterogeneous systems, with particular emphasis on functional material interfaces. By leveraging generative models together with insights from existing literature and software ecosystems, we aim to develop highly efficient computational frameworks capable of treating large-scale chemical and materials systems comprising hundreds of atoms.
